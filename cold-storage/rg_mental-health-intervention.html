Act IV — The Science

This is not speculation.
The literature is settled.

Three peer-reviewed findings sit at the foundation of this intervention. The exposure is documented. The harm is real. And — crucially — the remedy works.

The Exposure

Unemployment is a documented psychiatric risk factor

A fixed-effects analysis across 201 countries and five decades found significant, consistent associations between unemployment and anxiety, depression, and bipolar disorder — across every demographic studied. The effect is not marginal. It is structural. Each fruitless search session is a micro-dose of this exposure.

Yang et al. (2024) · Frontiers in Public Health · 201-country model, 1970–2020 · N > 3.2 billion person-years
The Compounding

The search itself deepens the wound

68% of job seekers in 2026 expect their search to take longer than any previous one. 54% report job insecurity significantly impacts their stress and mental health. Each failed session — each tab closed without a click — is not a neutral event. It is an additive exposure in a dose-response relationship with distress.

Robert Half Hiring Trends (Feb 2026) · APA Workplace Mental Health Survey (2025)
The Intervention

Even brief digital games measurably reduce distress

A controlled study with EEG and heart rate variability monitoring found that casual game play reduced depressive symptoms and physiological stress markers — with brainwave patterns biometrically confirming improved mood. Not self-reported. Measured. At the neurophysiological level. The intervention doesn't need to be clinical to be effective.

Russoniello et al. (2009) · Journal of CyberTherapy & Rehabilitation · RCT with EEG/HRV · Replicated 2011, 2014
The Evidence at Scale

Mental health interventions work for unemployed populations

A 2025 BMJ systematic review found that mental health interventions for unemployed populations demonstrate measurable value at both treatment and prevention levels — and may improve job readiness and re-employment likelihood. The intervention is not a consolation prize. It is potentially part of the solution.

BMJ Occupational & Environmental Medicine (2025) · Systematic review & meta-analysis · 1990–2024 literature

The recovered session creates value. What kind of value is also a test variable — ad revenue, lead generation, research partnerships, or direct relationships with the mental health industry itself.
Delivery — Test Variables
Step 03 is not a fixed decision — it is an experimental design space. These are the four primary delivery mechanisms we can test, each activating at the moment of exit intent:
🪟 Pop-up / Overlay
On-page modal before tab closes
Fires within the browser window on exit intent. Highest engagement potential — user is still on the page. Can display the micro-experience directly. Requires careful timing and a single clear dismiss path to feel supportive rather than intrusive.
Risk: low · Engagement: high · UX sensitivity: moderate
📄 Pop-under
Background window behind the browser
Opens a new window behind the active session — user discovers it after closing the main tab. Non-intrusive by design. Lower immediate engagement but zero disruption to the core search experience. The original proof-of-concept mechanism.
Risk: very low · Engagement: moderate · UX sensitivity: low
📧 Email Trigger
Post-session outreach to known users
For users with email addresses in our system (registration flows, existing lists), a triggered message can arrive within minutes of a failed session. "We saw you searching — here's something that might help." Enables ongoing relationship beyond the single session.
Risk: low · Engagement: variable · Requires: email permission
🧩 Embedded Content Unit
On-page module after zero-click session
After detecting that a user has browsed with no clicks, a subtle content module surfaces within the page: "Nothing matched today — try this." Purely in-page. No new windows. Most UX-safe option. Lower session time but highest platform trust preservation.
Risk: minimal · Engagement: low-moderate · UX sensitivity: minimal
Revenue — Test Variables
Step 04 is equally flexible. The recovered session can generate value through multiple channels — and these are not mutually exclusive. The right mix emerges from pilot data.
Display Advertising
Programmatic ad revenue
Google AdSense, Ezoic, Mediavine, Raptive, and others can monetize recovered sessions through standard display inventory. Platform choice affects CPM rates, fill rates, and revenue share. This is the simplest path to pilot — no new relationships required.
Complexity: low · Speed to revenue: fast
Mental Health Lead Gen
Direct partnerships with MH services
BetterHelp, Talkspace, Cerebral, Spring Health, and similar platforms pay significant amounts for qualified mental health leads — distressed adults who have demonstrated intent. Our exit population is exactly that. Cost-per-lead arrangements could substantially outperform CPM.
Complexity: moderate · Revenue upside: high
Platform Partnerships
MH tech companies need populations
The $153B market is full of companies that need exactly what we have: a validated, at-risk population for product testing, clinical research, outcomes measurement, and user acquisition. Licensing access to our population — anonymized, consented, instrumented — opens an entirely new B2B revenue category.
Complexity: high · Strategic value: highest
Note on Strategy
These are not competing paths — they are a phased roadmap. Start with programmatic ads (fast, zero-friction proof of concept). Validate the session economics. Then approach MH services with data in hand. Then, with a credentialed team and demonstrated outcomes, position for platform partnerships. Each phase compounds the last.
