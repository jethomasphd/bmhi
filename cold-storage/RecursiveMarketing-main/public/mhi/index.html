Why a job board asked you to pause

You came here to search for a job. We asked you to play a game first. That wasn't a gimmick. Here is why.

The weight of searching

Job searching is one of the most psychologically taxing routine activities in modern life. It combines uncertainty, rejection, financial pressure, and threats to identity into a single sustained experience.

The mental health consequences are well-documented. Umbrella reviews spanning 46 meta-analyses confirm that job loss and prolonged searching are associated with increased rates of depression, anxiety, and diminished well-being. The psychological burden persists across demographic groups and geographies, and compounds with search duration.

Most job boards ignore this entirely. They optimize for clicks and applications — not for the person sitting at the screen.

Small moments, measured impact

Over the past decade, researchers have documented a striking and counterintuitive pattern: brief interventions, sometimes lasting only minutes, can produce measurable and lasting improvements when delivered at the right moment through the right channel.

The evidence base has grown rapidly. Meta-analyses of digital mental health interventions now show effect sizes comparable to face-to-face therapy for mild-to-moderate symptoms. What these interventions share is a principle: timing and context matter more than dose.

The evidence

Nature Human Behaviour · 2024
Visual-spatial games interrupt rumination
A mega-analysis across multiple Tetris studies confirmed that visuospatial gameplay significantly reduces intrusive memories. The mechanism: spatial-cognitive tasks compete with the brain's ability to consolidate distressing imagery, temporarily interrupting the rumination loop.
Robust effect confirmed across pooled RCT data
Systematic review · Cell Reports Medicine
Controlled breathing and stress physiology
Brief structured breathing — even a single deliberate breath cycle — reduces physiological arousal and improves mood. Cyclic sighing outperformed mindfulness meditation in a Stanford RCT. Meta-analytic evidence confirms large effect sizes for breathwork on stress reduction.
Significant mood improvement from a single breath cycle
Meta-analysis · Casual games
Simple games reduce anxiety and improve mood
Systematic reviews of casual gaming show that simple, low-stakes games — puzzle, matching, and rhythm games — reduce state anxiety and improve affect, particularly when played in brief sessions. The effect is consistent across game types, suggesting the mechanism is cognitive engagement itself, not any specific game.
Consistent anxiety reduction across game types
Flow state research · 2024
Flow as a buffer against negative affect
When a game's challenge matches your skill level, you enter flow state — a condition of absorbed, effortless attention. Flow is associated with reduced self-referential thinking, the cognitive pattern that drives worry and rumination. Each game here is designed to invite flow through immediate challenge and clear feedback.
Flow correlates with reduced rumination and improved well-being

What the games do

Each game offered before your search targets overlapping mechanisms from the research above. You don't need to play them all — any one is a complete micro-intervention.

i
Visual-spatial engagement
Guiding falling blocks, tracking a ball, or aiming a shot requires visuospatial working memory — the same cognitive resource the brain uses to replay anxious scenarios. When that resource is occupied, the rumination loop is temporarily interrupted.
ii
Paced breathing
Before gameplay begins, you take a single guided breath. During play, a subtle breathing circle continues the rhythm. Even one deliberate breath cycle activates the parasympathetic nervous system, reducing physiological arousal. You may not have noticed. That's by design.
iii
Flow and cognitive absorption
Each game is calibrated to offer immediate, achievable challenge — the conditions that elicit flow state. Flow reduces self-referential thinking — the mental habit that sustains worry. A brief window of absorbed attention is enough to shift your cognitive baseline before you search.
iv
Brief positive anchoring
Words that appear during gameplay are micro-affirmations delivered at a moment of small accomplishment — anchoring a positive association at the point of action. Gamified delivery increases engagement with mental health content.

Why here

Because this is where people are when they need it most.

The core insight from single-session intervention research is that interventions work best when delivered at the moment of need, through channels people are already using. A job board is a point of convergence for a population under active psychological strain.

We are not a therapy app. We do not diagnose or treat clinical conditions. We are saying something simpler: if a postcard can measurably reduce suicide risk, a brief pause before a job search might matter more than it seems.

Your data

The game runs entirely in your browser. No gameplay data is collected, transmitted, or stored. No mental health information is inferred or recorded. The job search uses the same public federal jobs API as USAJobs.gov.
